## [intro] First Contact: The Door Approach

The first ten seconds at the door determine whether the homeowner stays or shuts down. Top reps approach with relaxed posture, no aggressive stance, and an energy that reads "neighbor" rather than "salesperson." The goal of the door approach is not to pitch — it's to earn five more seconds of conversation.

Best practice: Position yourself slightly off to the side of the door rather than directly in front of it. This is less confrontational and gives the homeowner the psychological sense that they can end the conversation at any time. Movement off the front step signals low pressure.

Open body language, a natural smile, and casual pace communicate safety before a single word is spoken. Leading with urgency or a product pitch kills rapport before it starts.

## [intro] Neighborhood Social Proof Opener

The neighborhood social proof opener is the most effective cold-open in pest control D2D. It borrows credibility from an existing neighbor relationship rather than asking the homeowner to trust a stranger.

Standard structure: "Hey, how's it going? I'm swinging by off of [street name] with [family name / company]. I work for them doing this stuff — it's been a lot of cobwebs and wasps [gesture up at eaves], even some ants starting to get inside. Since I'm here on the street, I can actually treat your home for half off."

Key mechanics:
- Street name grounds the visit in their physical reality (not a cold call)
- Specific pest reference (cobwebs, wasps, ants) shows observational awareness, not a memorized line
- "Half off" introduces value before resistance can form
- "I'm here on the street" implies scarcity — this is a street visit, not a routine sales call

This opener is designed to be interrupted. A confident rep expects the homeowner to interject and is not thrown by it.

## [intro] Moving the Homeowner Off the Step

A homeowner standing in their doorway is in a defensive position. Physical proximity to the exit means they can disengage with zero effort. The goal in the first 60 seconds is to naturally move them off the step and into a conversation rhythm — not by force, but by forward momentum.

Technique: Begin demonstrating by pointing at visible pest evidence (eaves, foundation, flower beds). The act of pointing draws the homeowner's eyes outward and breaks the "door guardian" posture.

As the rep shows observed evidence (cobwebs, ant trails, wasp nests), they begin moving naturally — the homeowner often follows. This is movement with purpose: "Let me show you what I'm seeing."

If the homeowner doesn't move: shift to the price sheet conversation as a lower-commitment step. "Let me at least leave you with a quick quote before I take off."

## [objection_handling] The "Not Interested" Response

"Not interested" is almost always a reflexive response, not a considered decision. The homeowner has not evaluated your offer; they are pattern-matching you to every other salesperson who has knocked.

Effective response: "Hey, totally get it. Let me just leave you with a quote in case you need us later." Then immediately pivot to a qualifying question: "What's your square footage?" or "Can I just take a look at your eaves real quick?"

The pivot works because:
1. It validates the objection (reduces defensiveness)
2. It asks for almost nothing (just information, not a decision)
3. It gives the rep physical movement toward the conversation

Do NOT argue with "not interested." Do not list features. Validate, pivot, ask a small question.

## [objection_handling] "I Already Have a Guy"

"I already have a guy" is one of the most common intro objections in pest control D2D. It is not a hard close — most homeowners who say this are open to comparison if handled well.

Correct response: "I figured — who do you have coming out?"

Then wait. Let them name the competitor.

Follow-up after they name the competitor: "Oh yeah, okay, awesome. I've actually had a few people switch over from [competitor] recently. We do a couple things differently — let me just leave you with a quick quote and I'll take off."

This technique works because:
- "I figured" signals that having service is normal, expected, and respectable
- Asking who they use shifts the homeowner into advisory mode (they're explaining, not defending)
- "A few people switch over from them" is soft social proof without attacking the competitor
- "I'll take off" releases pressure and makes the quote feel low-stakes

Do NOT disparage the competitor. The comparison sells itself once the rep shows the quote.

## [competitor_handling] Competitor Identification and Switchover

When a homeowner has an existing pest control provider, the goal is competitor identification followed by a switchover frame. Major pest control competitors in the market include Aptive, Terminix, and Orkin.

The switchover conversation follows a consistent pattern:
1. Identify the competitor: "Who do you have coming out?"
2. After competitor is named, ask how long: "How long have you been with them?"
3. Common answer: "A couple years." This is an opening.
4. Switchover frame: "Yeah, we've actually had a few people switch over from [competitor]. We do a couple things differently — [service differentiator]. Let me leave you with a quick quote."

The switchover frame is never a direct attack. It's a curiosity seed — "a couple things differently" — that invites comparison without confrontation. The differentiators (eco-friendly, no locked annual contract, bimonthly model) sell themselves once the quote is out.

If the homeowner has had the same provider for years, that's a strength to acknowledge: "Yeah, that makes sense — they've been around forever." Then pivot: "The thing is, we're offering something a little different this season because we're building our routes here."

## [price_framing] The Bimonthly Value Anchor

Pest control treatment frequency is a key decision driver for homeowners. The standard choices are monthly, bimonthly (every two months), and quarterly.

Bimonthly framing script: "So bugs actually hatch every 60 days. If you spray quarterly — every 90 days — you're leaving a 30-day gap where they can hatch and establish inside. Monthly is great but it's double the cost. Bimonthly hits both cycles so you're never behind."

This framing:
- Educates rather than sells
- Creates a logical anchor for bimonthly as the "smart" choice
- Makes quarterly feel like under-service and monthly feel like over-spending
- Positions the bimonthly plan as the value option at half the cost of monthly

Price anchoring sequence: Always lead with monthly price, then reveal bimonthly as "half the cost." This makes bimonthly feel like a deal regardless of absolute price.

## [service_value] Feature-Benefit-Soft Close Structure

The service walk through follows a Feature → Benefit → Soft Close pattern for each element. This builds value cumulatively before the final close.

Example sequence:

**Dewebbing:**
- Feature: "We do a full dewebbing — all the eaves, garage, foundation."
- Benefit: "Spiders come back to where the webs are. You remove the webs, you break the cycle."
- Soft close: "You've got some eaves over there that are pretty active, right?"

**Yard Treatment:**
- Feature: "We treat the perimeter — foundation, flower beds, any entry points."
- Benefit: "Most bugs come from the outside in. You treat the entry points, you don't get them inside."
- Soft close: "You've got a big yard — that's where it starts."

**Pet Question:**
- "Any dogs or cats?" — This is a soft close disguised as a question.
- The homeowner answers and implicitly enters a post-sale conversation about their specific property.

## [backyard_close] The Backyard Close Technique

The backyard close is a mid-conversation reset for homeowners who are interested but stalling. It involves physical movement, proof of need, and a price anchor reduction.

Structure:
1. "Can I take a quick look at your backyard? I want to show you what I'm seeing."
2. Walk to the backyard. Identify visible pest evidence: ant trails, wood rot, standing water, grub damage, webs.
3. Name the specific problem: "You've got some ant activity here, and this wood is starting to attract."
4. Reset the close with a price reduction: "Honestly, because I'm right here and I can fit you in, let me see what I can do."
5. Final anchor: "I need to fill this slot today — if I can get you at [price], does that work?"

Why it works: Physical movement breaks the "stuck at the door" pattern, evidence creates urgency based on their actual yard, and the slot urgency makes the decision time-sensitive without being manufactured.

## [objection_handling] Post-Pitch Objections — Real vs. Stalling

After the full pitch, objections fall into two categories: real objections and outside-the-sale distractions.

**Outside-the-sale distractions** (not real objections):
- "I need to check with my spouse" — real if the spouse is present; a stall if they're not
- "Let me think about it" — almost always a stall
- "I'll call you later" — a polite end
- "I'm tight on budget right now" — may be real or may be general reluctance

**Response to stalling:** "Totally. The only thing is this rate is specific to being on the same route — once I leave today I can't guarantee the same price. If you want, I'll knock on a few more doors and swing back in 15 minutes."

**Real objections** require the YES→Question→Address→CLOSE framework:
1. Acknowledge: "That's fair."
2. Question: Clarify the specific concern ("Is it the price, or is it the timing?")
3. Address: Directly respond to that specific concern
4. Close: Immediately re-ask for the appointment

Do not address both price AND service in one answer. Identify which fork it is, address only that one, then close.

## [objection_handling] Price vs. Service Objection Fork

When a homeowner says "I'm not sure," the rep must identify whether the resistance is about price or about service confidence.

**Price fork indicators:** "It's just a little expensive," "Maybe later in the year," "We're watching our budget"
**Service fork indicators:** "I'm not sure I need it," "I've never had a problem," "Our last company wasn't great"

**Price fork response:** "Here's the thing — the startup fee is waived and I'm throwing in the premium yard upgrade for free. So you're actually getting the full package at the basic rate. Does Tuesday or Thursday work better for you?"

**Service fork response:** "I get it. The thing is, most people don't see a problem until they have a problem — and then it costs a lot more to fix it. We're basically maintenance. What if we just do the initial treatment and you see how it goes?"

Never give both responses at once. Identify the fork, take only one branch.

## [closing] YES → Question → Address → CLOSE Framework

The core objection handling structure used by top D2D pest control reps:

1. **YES** — Agree with the homeowner's surface concern. Never argue. "That's fair." / "I get it."
2. **Question** — Ask to identify the real objection. "Is it the price, or more that the timing isn't great?" / "What's the main thing holding you back?"
3. **Address** — Respond to the specific objection only. Keep it to one focused response.
4. **CLOSE** — Immediately ask for the appointment. Do not let the conversation linger after addressing.

Common mistake: Reps address the objection but forget step 4. Homeowners don't close themselves. After every address, the rep must ask a specific closing question.

Close questions:
- "Does Tuesday or Thursday work better for you?" (Option close)
- "Let me get you on the schedule — what's your first name?" (Assumptive close)
- "If I can match that price, we're good to go?" (Conditional close)

## [closing] The Three Core Close Types

**1. Assumptive Close**
Treats the homeowner as if the decision is already made and moves directly to logistics.
"Alright, let me get you set up — I've got a slot at 3pm or 5pm Thursday. Which one works?"
Best for: Homeowners who are engaged and have stopped raising new objections.

**2. Assignment Close**
Assigns the homeowner a task or next step that implies commitment.
"So here's what we'll do — I'll put you down for Thursday and you'll get a confirmation text tonight. Does [address] look right?"
Best for: Analytical homeowners who want to feel like they're making an organized decision.

**3. Option Close**
Forces a choice between two positive options, both of which result in a sale.
"Would 3pm or 5pm work better for you?"
Never ask: "Does Thursday work?" — binary questions invite "no."
Always give two specific options.

## [closing] The Level Up Moment

The level up moment is a mid-conversation technique used when the homeowner is clearly engaged but still hasn't committed. It names the reality of the conversation to break the stalemate.

Script: "Hey — we've been talking for about 15 minutes. If you weren't at least a little interested, this conversation would've ended at the door. I'm not trying to sign you up for life. I'm just saying, give us a shot for a season, see how it goes. We don't lock you into a 24-month contract. You can cancel anytime. What do I need to do to make this easy for you?"

Why it works:
- It's honest about the conversation's length — which the homeowner already knows
- "I'm not saying marry me" reframes the commitment as minimal
- "What do I need to do" puts the homeowner in control and often reveals the real objection
- It signals confidence without desperation

Delivery must be calm and direct, not urgent or pleading.

## [reading_homeowner] Reading Engagement vs. Disengagement Signals

Homeowners signal their level of engagement continuously throughout the conversation. Reps who miss these signals either overstay their welcome or abandon conversations that could have converted.

**Positive engagement signals:**
- Sustained eye contact / asking follow-up questions
- Arms relaxed or uncrossed
- Moving closer to hear better
- Mentioning specific pest experiences ("we did have ants last summer")
- Asking about price without prompting
- Mentioning a spouse or partner as a decision co-maker

**Disengagement signals:**
- Glancing back inside the home repeatedly
- Short, clipped responses ("uh huh," "sure," "okay")
- Body turning slightly away
- Looking at their phone
- "I'll have to think about it" accompanied by backing up

**Skepticism signals (not the same as disengagement):**
- Crossed arms (neutral — indicates caution, not rejection)
- Asking "How long have you been with this company?"
- "I've been burned by companies like this before"
- Demanding to see credentials or a license

Skepticism can often be converted. Disengagement usually cannot without a pattern interrupt (humor, direct naming of the tension, or strategic exit and return).

## [psychology] Homeowner Decision Psychology at the Door

Homeowners at the door are in a heightened self-protective state. Research on door-to-door sales psychology identifies several patterns:

**First-impression bias:** Judgments about trustworthiness are made within 2-3 seconds based on appearance, posture, and energy — before any words are exchanged.

**Reactance:** When homeowners feel pressured, they push back harder. The more urgent or aggressive the pitch, the more resistance is triggered. Removing pressure (offering to "just leave a quote") paradoxically increases engagement.

**Social proof processing:** Homeowners heavily weight what their neighbors are doing. A neighbor reference is the fastest path to perceived legitimacy.

**Loss aversion:** Framing the service as "protection against an existing problem" outperforms "here's a great service." "You've already got some activity going on out here" is more powerful than "We do great pest control."

**Decision fatigue:** If a homeowner has had multiple D2D reps at their door in a short window, they are pre-resistant. Reading for this and acknowledging it directly ("I know you've probably had a few people come by today") can disarm it.

## [psychology] Handling Homeowner Distrust and Prior Bad Experience

A homeowner who has been burned by a prior pest control company is not a lost cause — they're often the easiest to convert if their prior bad experience is acknowledged correctly.

Common prior bad experiences:
- Service techs who didn't show up on time or at all
- Getting locked into a contract they couldn't exit
- Seeing no difference in pest activity after paying
- Feeling oversold at the door and under-delivered afterward

Acknowledgment frame: "I hear you. That's actually one of the reasons we don't do the long-term contract thing — because people got burned. We want you to stay because the service works, not because you're locked in."

This works because:
- It validates the bad experience without badmouthing the competitor by name
- It preemptively removes the most common fear (the contract trap)
- It positions the company as structurally different, not just "better"

## [service_value] Eco-Friendly Positioning and No-Contract Advantage

Two service differentiators that convert effectively in modern D2D pest control:

**Eco-friendly / pet-safe angle:**
"Everything we use is pet and kid safe — it's not the stuff from 30 years ago. Once it dries, your dog can be back out there in 20 minutes."
This is a closing accelerant for homeowners with pets or children, which is a large segment.

**No locked annual contract:**
"There's no annual contract. You're month to month. If you ever want to pause or cancel, you just call and it's done. No penalty."
This removes the primary psychological barrier to first-time signup. Most homeowners who say "I need to think about it" are actually afraid of being locked in.

When to deploy: After the homeowner has shown moderate interest but is stalling. Drop one or both of these as finishing moves rather than leading with them.

## [objection_handling] "We Just Moved In" / New Homeowner Situation

New homeowners are a high-conversion segment because:
- They don't know the pest history of the property
- They're in a "setting things up" mindset, not an "already have it handled" mindset
- They often don't have an existing service relationship

Opener adaptation: "Hey, are you guys new to the neighborhood? We're just getting routes set up in this area and wanted to check in with new homeowners specifically."

Then pivot: "A lot of people moving in don't know what the property's pest history is — the last owners might not have had regular treatment. We can actually do a free inspection and tell you what we're dealing with."

The free inspection offer is a foot-in-the-door technique. It creates an in-person visit with low commitment that almost always converts to service once the rep is on the property.

## [objection_handling] "We Do It Ourselves" (DIY Objection)

Some homeowners do their own pest control using store-bought products. This is not a hard block — it is an education opportunity.

Response: "Yeah, the stuff from the hardware store definitely helps. The thing is, a lot of those products don't have residual — they kill what's there but don't prevent the next generation. Our treatments have a 60-90 day residual, so you're actually building a barrier."

Follow-up: "What are you mainly dealing with? Ants? Spiders?" — This shifts from debate to consultation. Once the homeowner identifies their specific problem, they become emotionally invested in solving it.

## [intro] The Reframe From "Salesperson" to "Neighbor on the Route"

Top D2D pest control reps mentally reframe their role before approaching any door. They are not "salespeople." They are service professionals covering a route who happen to be extending a route rate to neighbors.

This reframe changes everything about delivery:
- Posture is relaxed, not tense
- Tone is informational, not persuasive
- Objections are expected and greeted with patience, not urgency
- "I'm just offering the same rate we're doing for the Hendersons" feels true because it is true

When the rep genuinely believes they are doing the homeowner a favor by offering the neighborhood rate, the homeowner feels it. Homeowners are extremely sensitive to incongruence — a rep who doesn't believe their own pitch transmits that uncertainty.
